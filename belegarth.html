Belegarth

Medieval foam combat — where the hits are real and the glory is eternal

What is Belegarth?

Belegarth Medieval Combat Society is a full-contact foam weapon fighting sport. Founded in 1979, it's one of the oldest live-action battle games in the US. Think medieval warfare — swords, shields, spears, bows — but made from foam and safety rules keep it fun and injury-light.

Events range from small weekly practices to massive multi-day wars with hundreds of fighters. The largest event, Geddon, draws fighters from across North America. There's no magic, no spellcasting, no class system — just raw tactical combat skill.

⚔ Weapon Classes

Every weapon class has its own feel, role, and skill ceiling. Here's the full breakdown.

⚔

Single Short

Under 18"

Daggers and short swords. Primarily used as a secondary weapon or for grappling range. Devastating in crowd situations where longer weapons get tangled.

  • Best paired with: Shield or second short
  • Skill floor: Low
  • Skill ceiling: Very High (off-hand mastery)
  • Typical role: Secondary, flanker
⚔

Single Sword

18"–48"

The foundation of Belegarth. Versatile, fast, and pairs beautifully with a shield. Most fighters start here and many never leave — mastery is a lifetime pursuit.

  • Best paired with: Shield, florentine, or alone
  • Skill floor: Low
  • Skill ceiling: Extremely High
  • Typical role: All-rounder, line fighter
□

Shield

Companion Equipment

Not just a passive blocker. Shields are offensive tools — bash, trap, and corner opponents. The shape matters: round shields favor mobility, towers favor coverage.

  • Pairs with: Any single weapon
  • Max size: 24"x36" (rectangular), 36" diameter (round)
  • Cannot be used as a weapon itself
  • Typical role: Anchor, defensive line
⸻

Spear / Javelin

48"–7' (spear) / throwable

The great equalizer. Reach advantages are enormous in Belegarth. A good spear fighter shuts down sword-and-board fighters before they can close the gap. Devastating in shield wall formations.

  • Best in: Line battles, choke points
  • Weakness: Close range, encirclement
  • Javelins can be thrown for one hit then discarded
  • Typical role: Line breaker, range control
⚔⚔

Florentine

Dual Wield

Two weapons, one fighter, infinite chaos. Florentine sacrifices the shield's passive defense for relentless offensive pressure. Requires excellent coordination and body mechanics to not hit yourself.

  • Any combo of short + sword, or dual swords
  • Skill floor: Medium-High
  • Skill ceiling: One of the highest in the game
  • Typical role: Flanker, chaos agent, duel specialist
⚯

Great Weapon

Two-Handed, 48"–7'

Greatswords, poleaxes, war hammers. Two-handed weapons hit hard, control large areas, and have reach that scares shields. You trade personal defense for area denial and raw power.

  • Blue (non-stabbing) or red (thrusting polearms) rules apply
  • Skill floor: Medium
  • Weakness: Close quarters, dual-wielders
  • Typical role: Line anchor, space controller
🏹

Archery

Ranged

Foam arrows from bows let you engage before lines clash. Archers can turn the tide of mass battles and are priority targets for a reason. Rock a sword as backup — you'll need it.

  • Arrows must use approved foam tips
  • Minimum draw weight and max poundage enforced
  • Body hits = death, limb hits = limb loss
  • Typical role: Support, line softener
⚪

Siege / Rocks

Thrown Weapons

"Rocks" are small foam balls that simulate thrown projectiles. They count as single hits to any body part and are incredible for disruption — throw into a tight shield wall to break formation.

  • Must be constructed to spec (foam, no rigid core)
  • Max 3 rocks per fighter typically
  • Often used by flankers and archers
  • Typical role: Disruption, gap creator

✎ Rules Deep Dive

🔸 Hit Locations

  • Head / Neck / Torso — Instant kill, drop immediately
  • Arm hit — Struck arm is "dead," must be held behind your back
  • Leg hit — That leg is dead, drop to your knee and fight from there
  • Hand (holding weapon) — Counts as arm hit
  • Foot / Foot while stationary — Counts as leg hit
  • Groin — Counts as leg hit

🔸 Shot Rules

  • Force required — Shots must have sufficient force; light grazes don't count
  • Flat of blade — Does not count as a valid hit
  • Edge/tip — Counts if sufficient force and contact
  • Hitting through your own weapon — Not valid
  • Self-calls — Honor system, call your own hits honestly
  • Disputes — Defer to the person hit, not the hitter

🔸 Garb & Equipment

  • Garb required — Pre-17th century style clothing at all events
  • No modern logos — Logos/branding must be covered
  • Weapon construction — Must pass safety check before use
  • Mandatory padding — Core must be foam, no rigid/metal materials
  • Color coding — Blue = slashing only, red = thrusting permitted, yellow = arrow

🔸 Conduct

  • No grappling — No grabbing opponents, weapons, or shields with hands
  • No shield bashing — Shield must not be used to hit
  • No head strikes from behind — When fighter is stationary/dead
  • Yield — A yielded fighter takes a death and steps out
  • Safety call — Anyone can call "HOLD" to stop combat for safety

📄 Getting Started

01

Find Your Realm

Realms are local Belegarth groups. Use the official realm finder to locate practices near you. Most realms welcome new fighters at any skill level.

02

Borrow Before Buying

Most realms have loaner weapons for new fighters. Don't buy gear until you know what style you want to fight. Try different weapons for a few sessions first.

03

Learn to Fall

When you take a death shot, drop gracefully. Don't fall on your sword arm, don't take others out. Practice controlled drops so you don't injure yourself.

04

Call Your Hits

Honor is everything. If you take a clean shot, call it out loud and go down. The community runs on mutual respect. Fighters who don't call shots don't last long.

05

Get Garb

Pre-17th century clothing is required at most events. Thrift stores, Renaissance faires, and online LARP shops are your friends. Simple linen tunics are cheap and look great.

06

Go to an Event

Local practices are fun, but big events like Geddon and War of Forbidding are life-changing. Hundreds of fighters, days of battles, and a community you'll never forget.
